Question #1

Schwulst advocates for individual websites over social media to reintroduce a sense of authorship and connection with what we create on the web. Rather than funnel creative thought and practice into the confines that large platforms such as Facebook, Instagram, Tumblr, etc. have created for us, we should be considering the relationship between the content of what we are trying to say or show and the ways we implement our tools of the web to relay those messages. Tying things back to Lialina and Seu’s writing on the web, Schwulst emphasizes that clarity need not be the primary purpose of a website, or an intended purpose at all. She implies that it’s the very nature of being less explicit with your message or subverting expectations by connecting the “architecture” of the website with the words, images, links, etc. that live within the space. By stating that “[websites] are both subject and object at once”, Schwulst reminds us that as creators of these sites (and particularly artists and designers), we have the opportunity for full control of the medium along with the message as we do within any other creative practice. Within her provided metaphors and repeated use of anthropomorphic vocabulary, she continually reminds us of the living nature of a website and the Internet; websites need to be nurtures, fed and cared for; they are interconnected with one another, sharing and communicating thoughts and moments; they can live and die; they “are living, temporal spaces”.

I absolutely agree with Schwulst here—while social media can provide some benefits (easy interconnectivity between large networks of people), it’s these very benefits that are gnawing away at a more critical, engaging, and fun way for us to use, implement, and live on the web. Rather than giving websites a single static duty to be a blank, easy-to-use vessel to transport our information, I believe in creating more active experiences that are thoughtful or just weird! I also believe that removing the “blackbox” (or at least distancing ourselves from it) that obscures the inner-workings of the sites we use every day of our lives creates more creative and empowered users. I particularly appreciate Schwulst’s point on stepping away from the “cloud” metaphor often associated with the web, and instead positioning it as, not out of our reach, but as a complex network made out of a bunch of little points—and those points are us!
